Autobiography

Natal Nostalgia

I was born on 10th of December, 2004 when my mother and I shared our first tears together, and in that moment, she became my first love. This might have made everyone (the reader) confused, because there was no need to mention her as my first love—everyone knew, and the word mother had always been everyone’s first love. Her name was Gelha Sahu. She was quite silent and trustworthy with a percent of 101%. Trust me, my maths was not weak—maybe you weren’t good at math.

Her nature was as simple as the maximum you could estimate. Though she lived in a village 15 km away from my city where I used to work, she cared for my health just like someone who lived outside the country, which others couldn’t.

Let me take you back to my childhood—when securing a good mark meant nothing to me, when no one forced me to get good marks as Sharmaji’s son was also a backbencher, when hide-and-seek was the ultimate thrill with top secret spots, when objects turned into imaginative playthings every day, when riding bicycles with friends meant a sense of freedom, when summer vacations meant carefree days, carefree life, when birthdays were more than just a date on the calendar.

At first, I was fond of Parle-G biscuits. My father brought two biscuits, one for breakfast and the second one which they gave me only when I promised, I wouldn’t be absent from school on that particular day. This was how they convinced me when I kept behaving badly and refused to attend classes. Wasn’t it ugly enough? No, perhaps it was not.

Bou (mother) said my father had tried a lot to make me sleep on his belly, but every time he failed. From my parents I learned that my siblings often slept on his belly, but I always tried to keep sleeping beside my Bou.

My village, Sankhapur, was known for its vibrant culture, warm-hearted people, and festivals, especially the Panchodola fair. It was the heart of the Panchayat, which included two villages—Kahalgaon and Sankhapur—both surrounded by greenery. When I was a child, it seemed that nearly everyone depended on farming.

My father was one of the well-respected farmers in our village. He was known for his strong build, hard work, and kindness. I remembered admiring his strong arms, which seemed to represent all the strength and love he put into his work. When I was about to finish Class 10, he stopped farming, but people still remembered him as one of the best.

Later on, he didn’t work but spent his time visiting our roadside properties, which we rented out to local shopkeepers selling things like fish, mutton, chicken, and other goods. Whenever I visited the village, I rarely looked at the shops, but my father often stopped by, reconnecting with the village he loved.

During my childhood, there was a teacher named Muni Apa who was famous for her strict teaching style. People used to tease her playfully, singing, Munni Badnaam Hui, Darling Tere Liye. One day, while my mother and I were returning from the fields, we stopped by her house. She was kind that day, offering me a banana, which I still remembered eating there. Not long after, I joined her class, where many of my friends—Tuin, Raja, Samir, Kanha, and Siba—were already learning.

Summer days back then felt like they would never end, filled with games and laughter. We played goli (marbles), cricket, long jump, chor-police, hide-and-seek with our secret hiding spots, and many more games. I was decent at most games, but in cricket, I had a bit of a reputation among my friends. Samir and I were always on the same team, with him as the captain. He had a natural ability to lead and stepped in whenever arguments arose, keeping the team united. After each game, he talked to me about my mistakes, offering advice on how to improve.

Guardians Unveiled

In my early years, two figures stood as the pillars of my upbringing: my father and my own sister Lucky. While many would look to their parents as the primary guides in life, for me, it was these two who shaped my understanding of the world in my childhood. They were the guardians who were tasked with the responsibility of teaching me how important education was in one’s life. Lucky, with her quiet strength, was the one who often didn’t speak much about the lessons she imparted, but her actions spoke louder than words. Her approach to teaching me was strict. She was the one who fought with me for a biscuit and fed me the one I hadn’t tasted by sacrificing her own piece. Papa, on the other hand, was a different story. He spoke louder and didn’t take much action that would let me pay attention to my studies, but he expected a lot.

Sumi Dee, whom I respected a lot and argued with the most, had a relationship with me that was often marked by misunderstandings, creating a gap that we both struggled to bridge. She held high expectations for me, especially when it came to helping her children with their studies—helping them in lessons, projects, reading, and finding appropriate answers. It wasn’t a request but an obligation in her eyes, one that I sometimes found difficult to fulfill. She cared for me since late 2020, treating me like her own son. She provided me with food, shelter, and the freedom to read and write whenever I wanted. Despite her kindness, there were moments when I doubted her intentions and argued, causing her stress. In November 2024, she even expressed her frustration to my parents, saying, “I can’t keep him with me anymore.” Yet, after our conversation that day, she asked me to stay.

But wait—I was getting ahead of myself. I meant only to introduce her. She was one of the most significant characters in this book, and no matter how much I wrote about her, it never felt enough. As I mentioned earlier, Sumi Didi was both strict and soft at the same time. She played a vital role in shaping my thoughts about good and bad habits. Before I ever heard of James Clear, she was the one guiding me. For instance, when I watched movies, her influence made me overthink them to the point where I stopped watching. I never smoked in my life, and that was largely because of her guidance. She shaped me into the kind of man I aspired to be.

Her husband, Santosh Kumar Sahoo, worked as a clerk in the SP office in Angul. He was a remarkable figure in our family. No one could match his simplicity and calm demeanor. Humble by nature, he had never consumed alcohol or even touched products like Safal Pan Masala in his entire life. He was a living example of all things good for our family.

They had two sons: Subhranshu, often called Subhi, and Shreyash, known as Shreya. Subhi was one of the brightest children in the family. He was incredibly disciplined about his studies, hated missing school, and was always engrossed in something productive—whether it was drawing, reading, or completing his homework. Despite his seriousness about academics, he was also funny and mischievous. Shreya, on the other hand, was slightly less focused on studies but had the potential to excel. He grasped concepts well but lost interest quickly, which made him forget things over time. I often helped him with his homework and other schoolwork, and I heard my fair share of complaints about his studies. Whenever he got distracted, I noticed it and reported it to my sister.

I felt a great sense of responsibility for him. If I suspected he was doing something he shouldn’t, I worried endlessly, fearing the consequences and blaming myself for not taking better care of him. Thankfully, he rarely got into serious trouble and seldom argued with me. When I spent time with him, I tried to strike a balance between play and study. Sometimes, I even had to discipline him to ensure he stayed on track, but all of it was for his betterment.

I didn’t talk much with Lipi Dee, and I didn’t have many childhood memories with her. Her husband, Jogendra Sahu, whom we called Rulu, was a software engineer and Python developer. They lived in Bangalore and had a son, Duhitesh, whom we lovingly called Piyush. Piyush was just as mischievous as Subhi. He loved video games, and I had seen his mother’s phone packed with them. He was also very fond of cars and toys. Sumi Didi often engaged him during video calls by showing him toys and encouraging him to chat.

Rulu Uncle was a significant figure in our family, and my parents had high expectations of him. We all believed he might help me secure a job in the future. He was known for his strict nature and didn’t hesitate to teach you a lesson if you had made a mistake or failed to follow his advice.

My grandfather was married to two women, but such was the distance this generation had created that I didn’t even know their names while writing this book. One night, I asked my mother, and she told me their names: Sankar Sahu and Uttama Sahu. My grandfather and his two wives were a part of a history I never witnessed—I had never seen my first grandmother or my grandfather. After their time, I had Asha Sahu, whom I called “Bantala Aii” because she lived with my elder father in Bantala back then. It had been 6–8 years since she also joined her husband in heaven. Her passing coincided with Prathamastami, a day that now carried a deep sense of loss for our family. It was believed that we boys, and the generations to follow, would not celebrate this day unless a child was born on the same day, breaking the cycle of grief.

School Friends

Family felt incomplete without friends. Samir was my all-time best friend, while Siba had a passion for math. Dinesh was our leader during primary school, and Raja was known for causing disturbances in and out of class. Krushna had excellent batting skills, and Pritam was focused on earning and saving money. Siku was my second favorite friend in school. Though I had many friends, these played a special role in my life.

Among my female friends, Tuin stood out as the class topper from the beginning until the end of school. Kajal held many memories filled with silly, childish moments. Titly, Rajashree, Aliva, Sarita, and Sibani were also there for me from primary school to matric. Everything felt good until tragedy struck— my best friend Samir passed away in September 2024 due to sickling. That was the darkest day of my life, one I never forgot. Stick with me to uncover what really happened.
